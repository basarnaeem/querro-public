# Who It's For

## The first customer segment

The platform is built first for **US middle-market boutique investment banks** — firms running $50 million to $2 billion transactions across sell-side M&A, buy-side M&A, debt capital raises, restructuring, equity capital markets, private equity fund formation, and standalone advisory mandates.

This segment was chosen for four reasons.

**Volume.** Middle-market boutiques close 70% of US transactions by count. Per banker, throughput is high — four to twelve closed transactions per year per senior banker is normal — which means per-engagement efficiency gains compound rapidly.

**Pain.** The kickoff workflow is genuinely broken at this tier. Bulge-bracket banks have internal knowledge management systems and dedicated deal-prep teams; boutiques run on senior partners' memory and a generic template. The variance in question quality across kickoff meetings at a single mid-market firm is enormous, and that variance is the cost.

**Buying authority.** Boutique firms have decentralized technology procurement. A managing director can authorize a software pilot without a six-month committee process. The sales cycle is measured in weeks, not quarters.

**No internal AI engineering capacity.** Mid-market firms have neither the engineering teams to build this in-house nor the desire to. They will buy.

The named target list, in roughly descending order of fit: Houlihan Lokey, William Blair, Harris Williams, Lincoln International, Raymond James, Stifel, Piper Sandler, Robert W. Baird, Cowen, Capstone Partners, and approximately 200 to 300 firms in the next tier.

## What a deal team gets

A deal team using Querro gets four things that do not exist in any other tool today.

**A question set that is tailored to their exact deal.** A sell-side mandate on a regional bank with a Shariah-compliant capital structure receives a different questionnaire than a buy-side mandate on a SaaS company. The questions are not a generic template with some fields greyed out. They are a composed set derived from the deal's mandate type, the target's industry and business model, the applicable jurisdictions, and any special situations that apply.

**A structured engagement record from the first meeting.** Every answer is captured as typed, structured data — not narrative notes. This means it is machine-readable, auditable, and reusable as the source for every downstream output.

**Every downstream output from one data layer.** Briefing memos, risk registers, sector research, country research, financial model inputs, and eventual pitchbooks all flow from the same structured record. There is no re-entry, no re-formatting, no reconciling two documents that describe the same client.

**A quality floor across the firm.** When every deal team uses the same structured intake for the same deal type, the variance in question quality across the firm collapses. Junior analysts cannot forget a question class. Senior bankers do not spend time correcting what was missed.

## How the platform is priced

Querro is priced as a tiered SaaS product. Pricing is calibrated to be a fraction of the annual cost of one analyst, making the ROI calculation straightforward for a managing director: if the platform saves ten analyst-hours per engagement and a firm runs thirty engagements per year, the math closes at any reasonable per-hour cost assumption.

Specific pricing figures are available through direct inquiry rather than this document, as pricing evolves with the product.

## Expansion path

The platform extends naturally beyond investment banking. The underlying architecture is designed so that adding a new profession — law firms, management consulting, audit and risk advisory, private equity due diligence — requires new content rather than new engineering. The underlying engine, the data model, and the user interface do not change. Only the content library expands.

The expansion sequence is not public, but the architecture explicitly supports it, and the investment banking beachhead is chosen in part because it generates the highest-quality structured engagement records from which the adjacent professions can learn.
